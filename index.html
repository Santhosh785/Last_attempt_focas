How to Make Sept '26
Your Last CA Inter Attempt

A practical comeback guide for students who didn't clear May '26 — built around what actually changes the outcome the second time around.

If you're reading this within a week of your result, good. The first two weeks after a result decide more than people realise — not because of anything magical, but because this is when most students either drift into denial/avoidance, or quietly start the next attempt with a clear head. This guide is built for the second path.

1Reflect on Your Mistakes

Before you open a single book again, understand exactly why the last attempt didn't work. Repeating without this step usually means repeating the same mistakes.

Apply for RTI on your answer sheets

Your evaluated copies are the single most underused resource after a result. RTI shows you exactly where marks were lost — presentation, working notes, step-marking, or genuine knowledge gaps. Don't guess what went wrong; read it off the paper.

Evaluate your previous preparation honestly

  • Did you finish the syllabus before the exam, or were you still covering new chapters in the last 10 days?
  • How many full mock tests did you write under exam conditions before the actual exam?
  • Were your low scores from weak concepts, weak presentation, or weak time management in the exam hall?
  • Which paper(s) pulled your aggregate down — was it one weak paper or an across-the-board shortfall?

See what successful students actually do differently

Talk to 2–3 people who cleared this attempt. Almost always it isn't "smarter," it's: fewer new resources, more revision cycles, more written mock tests, and a fixed daily routine they didn't break. Borrow the routine, not the resources.

2Make the Group Decision

This is the most consequential decision you'll make this attempt — and it should be made on hours available, not on emotion.

The maths that matters:
First attempt at a paper ≈ 120–180 hours of preparation.
Repeat attempt at the same paper ≈ 60–80 hours — but only if you treat it as starting from zero. The clock resets every attempt; partial memory from May is not a shortcut.

Be honest about the hours you can realistically commit on a normal day, not your best day. Decide one of three paths and commit — don't keep re-deciding every week:

  • Group 1 only — Advanced Accounting, Corporate & Other Laws, Taxation
  • Group 2 only — Cost & Management Accounting, Auditing & Ethics, Financial Management & Strategic Management
  • Both Groups — all six papers in one attempt

3Start Solving Now

Reading and re-reading chapters feels productive but doesn't build exam fitness. Writing does. Start within the first week — not after "one more revision round."

  • Pick up the question bank / RTP / MTP for each paper and start solving daily, even before your revision is "complete."
  • Write at least one full 3-hour mock under exam conditions every week from week 3 onward — same timing, same pressure, no phone.
  • Get every mock evaluated or self-evaluate strictly against the ICAI suggested answers, not just "I knew this."

5Making This Attempt Different

A study plan alone doesn't change outcomes — the habits around it usually do.

  • Tell one person your group decision and your weekly target out loud — accountability beats willpower.
  • Protect sleep and one full off-block per week; burnout in week 4 costs more than it saves in week 1.
  • Track attempts, not hours — "3 mocks written" is a better weekly metric than "20 hours studied."
  • Revisit your RTI findings every 3 weeks — check if the same mistake is creeping back in.
  • Join a structured group (workout batch, study group, or mentor check-ins) — preparing for a repeat attempt alone is the single biggest risk factor for drifting.
  • Treat May '26 as data, not identity. One attempt's result describes a result, not your ability to clear CA Inter.
